A kinetic light system built for product stories, not card walls.

This version is intentionally closer to the motion and visual pacing of the Codex homepage: one dominant scene, a moving light field, and a page structure that guides the eye through product proof instead of dropping everything into equal modules.

Page Flow

Three moves that make this feel more like a real product page.

The reference point here is the movement and pacing of the Codex homepage, not a literal copy. The same principle is applied to a new light palette and a more editorial page rhythm.

01
Directional hierarchy

Lead with one dominant visual field.

The hero should behave like a scene. A large statement, one product surface, and one ambient light field create a clear entry point. That is stronger than dropping several equal cards above the fold.

02
Ambient motion

Move the atmosphere, not the reading surface.

The cursor shifts light, mesh, and depth layers behind the content. This creates a premium sense of motion without making panels feel unstable or decorative.

03
Containment discipline

Keep panels rare enough that they still mean something.

Cards are no longer the whole page language. Large figures, slender rails, and quiet separators do most of the work. Panels appear only when detail actually needs containment.

Fit Notes

When this version is the right answer.

Pick this if the product needs to feel premium, directional, and alive before the user even starts reading details. It is strongest when story and software need to coexist on the same page.

Landing pages AI products Feature launches
  1. Use it when there is one main message to deliver before the user gets into dense detail.
  2. Use it when ambient motion can help establish brand value without getting in the way of interaction.
  3. Avoid it when the page is mostly tables, administration, or repetitive forms. In those cases, `v2` or `v3` will be more trustworthy.
